Botanical Tutorial

How to Press Flowers at Home

A simple step-by-step guide to drying and pressing garden flowers before casting them in resin.

Step 1: Selecting and Preparing Blooms

Not all flowers press the same. Thick, moisture-heavy flowers like full roses, orchids, and lilies can rot if pressed flat without care. Delicate, thin petals like daisies, pansies, cosmos, and ferns are ideal for traditional pressing.

Harvest flowers in the morning after the dew has fully dried.
Remove bulk stem parts and backing leaves using micro shears.
Ensure there is no surface moisture on the petals to prevent mold.

Step 2: Traditional Wooden Press Method

Ideal for creating flat flowers suitable for bezels or framed prints. You will need corrugated cardboard, watercolor blotting paper, and a heavy wooden book or screw-tight press.

Layer: Cardboard → Blotting Paper → Flower → Blotting Paper → Cardboard.
Arrange petals carefully so they do not overlap unless desired.
Press flat under weights for 10-14 days. Avoid opening early!

Step 3: Silica Curing (3D Flower Preservation)

Required if you want your flowers to maintain their natural, three-dimensional cup shape (like roses or carnations). Silica gel crystals rapidly draw out moisture while preserving the structure.

Fill an airtight container with 1 inch of fine silica gel crystals.
Place the flower face-up and gently spoon crystals over the petals.
Seal and leave for 4-7 days. Flowers will emerge paper-dry and sturdy.

Step 4: Casting Cautions in Resin

Once fully dried, your botanicals are ready to be cast. Ensure you use protective goggles, nitrile gloves, and work in a well-ventilated space.

Seal dry petals first with an acrylic spray to prevent translucent spots.
Pour resin in thin layers, curing each layer before adding the next.
Use a heat gun briefly over the liquid to burst rising micro-bubbles.
