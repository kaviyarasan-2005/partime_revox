The Secret to Perfect Ice Cream Texture: A Deep Dive into Small-Batch Churning

Clara Montague
June 10, 2026 8 min read

There is a particular moment — the instant a perfectly tempered scoop meets a warm waffle cone — that defines why we do what we do. That moment is the result of thousands of micro-decisions made in a kitchen that begins its day at 4:30am, long before the first customer walks through our door.

Today, for the first time, we're opening that kitchen door and sharing the craft that goes into every single batch.

The Overrun Question

Every ice cream has an "overrun" — the percentage of air churned into the base during freezing. Commercial producers routinely push this to 100% or higher, doubling the volume with air to lower costs. We cap ours at 20–25%. This means denser, heavier ice cream with far more flavour per spoonful.

The difference is immediately perceptible. Pick up a pint of supermarket ice cream and a pint of ours — ours will be nearly twice as heavy. That weight is real cream, real fruit, real flavour.

 Clara pouring the morning's first Jersey cream into our copper churning vessel

Temperature Precision

Ice cream texture is governed by crystal formation. Faster churning at lower temperatures creates smaller ice crystals — and smaller crystals mean silkier texture. We chill our base to exactly -3°C before it enters the churner, and we never let the mixture exceed -6°C during the process.

"Great ice cream isn't made by accident. It's made by obsessing over every variable — from the temperature of the cream to the size of the crystal."

We use a Cattabriga Effe 4 MK — an Italian batch freezer from Emilia-Romagna, the same region that gave us Parmigiano Reggiano and prosciutto di Parma. The Italians understand that great food cannot be rushed, and this machine embodies that philosophy.

Our Key Ingredients

  • Jersey cream from a single Devonshire farm — 5.2% butterfat vs 3.6% standard
  • Pasteurised free-range eggs — for richness and natural emulsification
  • Unrefined cane sugar — lower glycaemic index, more complex sweetness
  • Zero stabilisers, gums, or emulsifiers of any kind
  • Whole vanilla pods — never extract, never artificial flavouring
 The finished Tahitian Vanilla Bean — visible seeds from hand-scraped pods

The Ageing Stage Most Brands Skip

After mixing our base, we age it for 12–24 hours at 4°C. This step — called "maturation" in French — allows the proteins and fats to fully hydrate, and the flavours to marry and deepen. Most commercial producers skip this entirely. We consider it non-negotiable.

The result? A scoop with remarkable stability. Even on a warm summer's day, ice cream holds its shape and structure for longer than you'd expect — not because of additives, but because the structure of the ice cream itself is more coherent.

 The morning's first batches set in our Carrara marble display

Why Small Batch Matters

Each batch we make is 5 litres maximum. This isn't a marketing decision — it's a quality one. Smaller batches allow us to taste and adjust at every stage. They allow us to source the most exceptional seasonal ingredients, even when availability is limited. And they allow us to say with complete honesty: every scoop you eat was made by one of our team of six artisans, today.

The next time you visit us, ask which batch your scoop came from. We'll tell you who made it, when, and what farm the dairy came from. That transparency is as important to us as the taste itself.
